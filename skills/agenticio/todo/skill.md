# Todo

## The List That Never Gets Done

Most people have a to-do list. Many people have several — a notebook, a phone app, a sticky note on the monitor, a mental list that runs in the background consuming attention whether or not anything is being done about it. The items accumulate faster than they are completed. The list grows until it becomes a source of anxiety rather than a tool for clarity, at which point it is abandoned and a new one is started, cleaner and more optimistic, which will follow the same trajectory.

The problem is not the list. The problem is what the list is missing.

A list of tasks is not a system for getting things done. It is a storage mechanism. What transforms a list into a system is the logic that determines which item deserves attention right now, given your current context, your available time, your energy level, and the actual consequences of doing or not doing each thing. Without that logic, a long list produces paralysis as reliably as it produces progress.

This skill provides the logic.

## Capture Without Friction

The first failure point of every task system is capture. An idea arrives, a commitment is made, a responsibility is accepted — and the system for recording it requires enough steps that the thought is gone before the recording is complete.

The skill accepts tasks in any form, from any context, without requiring you to categorize, prioritize, or format them at the moment of capture. A fragment is enough. "Call the accountant before Thursday." "Figure out the situation with the supplier." "Something Sarah said about the contract — look into it." "Don't forget the thing for Tuesday."

The skill extracts the task, identifies any deadline or dependency embedded in the description, and holds it reliably until you are ready to engage with it. The capture is designed to be faster than the thought that generated it.

## The Priority Question

Not all tasks are equal. Most task systems treat them as if they were — a flat list where everything sits at the same level of urgency and importance regardless of actual consequence. The result is a list that gets worked from the top, or from the most recently added, or from whatever feels most manageable in the moment — which rarely corresponds to what actually matters most.

The skill applies a consistent logic to prioritization that distinguishes between four types of tasks. Things that are urgent and important — deadlines with real consequences, commitments to people who are waiting, problems that compound if not addressed. Things that are important but not urgent — the work that shapes the future rather than managing the present, chronically deprioritized because nothing bad happens today if they are deferred again. Things that feel urgent but are not actually important — requests that arrive with other people's urgency attached, tasks that create the sensation of productivity without advancing anything that matters. Things that are neither urgent nor important — the tasks that belong on a someday list rather than a daily one.

Understanding which category a task belongs to changes how you engage with it. The skill makes this categorization explicit rather than leaving it to the snap judgment made under the pressure of a full inbox.

## Context and Energy

The right task at the wrong moment is the wrong task.

A task that requires sustained focus and original thinking is the wrong task for the fifteen minutes between two meetings when your cognitive reserves are already depleted. A task that requires a phone call is the wrong task when you are in a space where you cannot make one. A task that requires access to a specific system is the wrong task when you are away from the device that has it.

The skill learns your contexts — the different environments and energy states that characterize your day — and surfaces tasks that are executable given your current situation. When you have two hours of uninterrupted time and high focus, it surfaces the work that benefits most from those conditions. When you have twenty minutes and low energy, it surfaces the small, mechanical tasks that can be completed without much cognitive cost. When you are mobile, it surfaces the tasks that can be done from a phone.

This is not a minor optimization. It is the difference between a system that makes decisions for you and a list that requires you to make decisions from it.

## Commitments to Others

The tasks that have the highest interpersonal cost when dropped are not the ones on your personal list. They are the commitments made to other people — the deliverable you promised by Thursday, the follow-up you said you would send, the thing someone is waiting on before they can move forward.

The skill tracks these separately. When you make a commitment in conversation — "I'll get that to you by end of week," "let me look into that and come back to you," "I'll send you the details tomorrow" — the skill captures it with the person's name, the commitment, and the deadline if one was stated. These items surface in your morning briefing as their deadlines approach. They do not fall through the gaps that open between a busy day and the list you meant to check.

It also tracks what others have committed to you — the deliverable someone else promised, the response you are waiting for, the information that should have arrived by now. When these go overdue, the skill drafts a follow-up for your approval rather than leaving you to remember that the follow-up is needed.

## The Weekly Review

A task system without a regular review is a system that gradually fills with items that are no longer relevant, commitments that have been superseded, and projects that have been quietly abandoned without being officially closed.

The weekly review is the maintenance that keeps the system honest. The skill guides you through it in whatever time you have — a thorough version when you have thirty minutes, an abbreviated version when you have ten. It moves through your lists systematically: closing what is complete, updating what has changed, deferring what genuinely belongs later, and deleting what no longer matters.

The review is also where new commitments get captured from the week that passed — the things you agreed to in meetings, the tasks that emerged from conversations, the items that lived in your head all week and never made it onto any list. After the review, the system reflects your actual situation rather than an outdated version of it.

## Projects vs Tasks

A task is a single action. A project is any outcome that requires more than one action to achieve.

Most people manage projects as if they were tasks — a single item on the list that represents something considerably more complex than a single action. The item sits on the list for weeks because there is no single action associated with it, only a vague weight that grows heavier each time it is seen without being addressed.

The skill distinguishes between tasks and projects explicitly. For every project, it identifies the very next physical action — the specific, concrete thing that can be done right now to move the project forward. That action goes on the active task list. The project itself goes on the projects list, where it is reviewed weekly to ensure that it always has a next action associated with it.

A project without a next action is a project that is not moving. The skill makes sure every project is moving.

---

## Someday and Maybe

Not every idea deserves immediate attention. Some things are worth capturing without committing to acting on them now — a book to read eventually, a trip to take someday, a project to start when the current ones are finished, an idea that might be worth pursuing when the timing is right.

These belong on a someday list, not the active task list. The active list should contain only things you are genuinely committed to doing in the current period. Everything else is noise that makes the signal harder to find.

The skill maintains a someday list separately from the active list. It surfaces items from it during the weekly review so that things do not sit there indefinitely without ever being reconsidered — either moved to active when the time is right or deleted when it becomes clear that the time will never be right and that is fine.

---

## The End of the Day

A task system that does not have a closing ritual is a task system that follows you into your evenings and weekends. The list is always there. The undone items are always visible. The transition from working to not working never quite completes.

The skill builds a simple end-of-day routine. A review of what was completed, what was not and why, and what the first action is tomorrow morning. A capture of anything that came up during the day that needs to be processed. A deliberate closing of the system for the day.

The list will be there tomorrow. Tonight it can wait.
